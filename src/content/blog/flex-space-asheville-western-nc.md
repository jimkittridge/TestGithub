Asheville's industrial market is tight. The Asheville MSA has a 2.9% vacancy rate, compared to 7.7% nationally. That means if you're looking for flex space or small warehouse here, you need to know where to look and you need to move fast.

Here's the real situation: Asheville itself doesn't have much industrial space. The city's hemmed in by mountains. The usable industrial inventory is south of the city, primarily in Fletcher, Arden, and along Highway 64 toward Hendersonville. If you're searching for "warehouse near Asheville," you're probably looking at a 15 to 20 minute drive from downtown.

## Where the Space Actually Is

Fletcher is the main corridor. You'll find industrial parks clustered near the airport and along I-26 south of Exit 40. Arden sits just south of Fletcher, with similar corridor access and slightly lower rents. Hendersonville is further out, roughly 30 minutes from Asheville proper, but it's another option if Fletcher's full.

The older Asheville and south Asheville areas have scattered smaller buildings, mostly converted retail or light industrial from the 1980s and 90s. Some are decent. Others have foundation issues or weird column spacing. The newer flex parks in Fletcher are newer stock, typically built in the last 10 to 15 years.

## Pricing in a Tight Market

NNN (base rent plus your share of taxes, insurance, and common area maintenance) runs $18 to $22 per square foot annually in Fletcher and Arden for newer space. Older buildings in Asheville proper or Hendersonville might be $15 to $18 NNN, but you're usually trading condition or location for that discount.

A 2,000 SF unit at $20 NNN costs you about $33,000 a year in base rent, plus another $400 to $600 a month for NNN, roughly $3,000 to $3,500 a month total. That's the baseline you should expect.

## Why Mountain Geography Matters

Asheville's industrial market is shaped by three factors: limited flat land, mountain access constraints, and the fact that I-26 is the spine of everything industrial in Western NC. Anything south of Asheville has I-26 access. Anything north of Asheville or in West Asheville is harder to service and usually commands a premium precisely because industrial users don't want to be there.

If you're a contractor, distributor, or service business, I-26 access matters. It cuts your drive time to jobsites throughout the region. Without it, you're adding 10 to 30 minutes to every trip to Greenville, Spartanburg, or Charlotte.

## Who Leases Here

Contractors dominate Fletcher and Arden. General contractors, concrete crews, electrical shops, HVAC installers, and plumbers need bay space, electrical, and dock access. You'll also see food producers serving the tourism economy (small batch sauces, baked goods for hotels and restaurants), distributors (parts, tools, supplies), and equipment rental shops.

Tourism is a big part of the Asheville economy. Hotel maintenance, restaurant equipment repair, event staging, and seasonal labor require space. Much of that space is in Fletcher or Arden, close to I-26.

## What to Know Before You Lease

Lease length matters in a tight market. Landlords want longer terms; they can afford to be selective. A 1 year lease will cost more per month than a 3 or 5 year lease. Plan for $500 to $1,000 to get your space move-ready (paint, electrical work, concrete repairs).

Electric capacity varies wildly in Asheville. Some older buildings have 100 amp service. Newer flex parks usually have 200 amp. If you need 200 amps and a building maxes at 100, you're dealing with a utility upgrade, which takes months and costs thousands.

Clear height is another variable. 14 feet is a baseline. 16 feet is better. 20 feet is premium. If you're storing palletized inventory or running machinery, clear height affects your usable space and your rent.

Parking and loading apron size matter if you're running delivery trucks, service vans, or contractor crews. The larger flex parks in Fletcher have plenty of both. Older buildings in Asheville sometimes don't.
