A contractor in West Asheville is paying $250 per month for a climate-controlled 10x20 storage unit 4 miles from the jobsite. Inside are his tools, some equipment, and materials. The problem: when he needs something, he drives to the unit, unlocks it, grabs what he needs, and drives to the jobsite. That's a 30 minute round trip twice a day. At two trips a day, five days a week, that's 10 hours per week driving to a storage unit.

He's not alone. A lot of Asheville-area contractors, small equipment operators, and service businesses start with storage units. They're cheap, easy to rent, and you don't need much paperwork. But as the business grows, storage units become a pain.

The math is starting to change them. A 2,000 SF flex space in Fletcher rents for $3,300 to $3,500 per month. That sounds like a lot compared to a $250 storage unit, but a $250 unit is only 200 square feet and it doesn't have power, parking, or a door you can back a truck through.

## The Storage Unit Reality

Storage units are climate-controlled, dust-free, and secure. If you're storing finished goods, equipment that needs protection, or tools that need to stay organized, that matters. Climate control is about 40 percent of your monthly cost.

But storage units have hard constraints: no power, no loading equipment, no parking for crew or client vehicles, limited hours (usually 6 AM to 6 PM), and one small man door.

For a contractor, these constraints are crippling. You need electrical to run tools, charge batteries, or keep equipment warm in winter. You need parking so your crew can show up, grab tools, and head to the jobsite. You need hours outside 9 to 5 because construction happens before sunrise and after sunset. You need a door big enough to move equipment through.

## The Comparison: Cost and Reality

A 10x20 climate-controlled storage unit rents for $150 to $300 per month in Asheville (depending on facility and climate control). That's 200 SF.

A 1,000 SF flex bay in Fletcher rents for $1,600 to $1,800 per month all-in. That's 5 times the space for roughly 6 to 8 times the cost.

But the comparison is misleading. You're not just getting more space. You're getting:
- Electrical (100 to 200 amps)
- 24/7 access
- Parking for 2 to 4 vehicles
- A roll-up or drive-in door (not a man door)
- No humidity, temperature swings, or dust
- The ability to run equipment
- Professional security and maintained common areas

The real value is in eliminating wasted time. A contractor using a storage unit is probably spending 5 to 10 hours per week driving to grab tools. At $50 per hour billable rate, that's $250 to $500 per week in lost productivity.

A flex space at $1,800 per month costs you $45 per work day. Saving 1 hour per day in drive time gets you back $50 in billable capacity, plus 5 hours per week to spend on actual work instead of logistics.

## Mountain Geography and Distance

Asheville's geography makes this worse. The city is hemmed in by mountains. Industrial space (where contractors want to be) is south in Fletcher and Arden. Contractors are often working in Asheville proper, West Asheville, or north toward Weaverville.

A contractor with a storage unit in South Asheville (because it's cheaper) and a jobsite in downtown Asheville is 10 to 15 minutes each way. A 7 AM start time means 6:45 AM storage run, then 7:15 AM jobsite arrival. One forgotten tool or supply means another trip.

A flex bay in Fletcher is 20 minutes from downtown but you've got power, parking, and no lost time. You load the truck the night before and go. The extra commute time is less than the time saved on logistics.

## Asheville Storage Unit Costs

Climate-controlled storage runs $150 to $300 per month in Asheville depending on the facility and unit size. Non-climate storage (basically a shipping container) runs $75 to $150. For a contractor who needs climate control, you're at $150-250 per month minimum.

If you're renting two units (one for tools, one for materials), you're at $300 to $500. That approaches 20 percent of a flex space monthly cost but gives you none of the functionality.

## Electricity: The Killer

Storage units have no electricity. A contractor without power can't:
- Charge batteries overnight
- Run space heaters in winter to prevent tool rust or battery drainage
- Run dehumidifiers if the building gets damp
- Run a power tool to check equipment
- Keep a fridge or coffee maker for the crew

Upgrading to a unit with electric hookup is expensive and usually not available. Most storage facilities don't offer it. You're paying hundreds per month for a space you can't actually use for the work.

A flex bay with 200 amp electrical lets you run whatever you need. Your tools charge overnight. Your equipment stays functional. Your crew can get coffee before heading to a 5 AM jobsite.

## Parking and Crew Access

A storage facility has maybe 8 to 10 parking spots for 20 to 40 units. Good luck if your crew shows up at the same time. Flex spaces in industrial parks have dedicated parking for each tenant. Two to four vehicles per bay, clearly marked.

This matters in Asheville because weather. Winter mornings are cold and often icy. Your crew needs to park close, grab tools, and go. They're not walking across a lot looking for space.

## The Helene Factor

Post-Helene, contractor demand is high and storage units are full. Some facilities in Asheville have waiting lists. If you need space now and a storage unit is your backup, you might not get one. Flex space is tight too (2.9% vacancy), but [Get Flex Space](/) has availability and can move you in within 2 weeks.

## When Storage Still Makes Sense

Storage units work if you're storing finished goods, seasonal inventory, or equipment that doesn't need power. A food producer storing 500 units of product. A seasonal business storing winter stock in summer. Someone keeping archive equipment that doesn't run.

Storage doesn't work if you're a contractor, service business, or equipment operator running a daily operation. The time cost outweighs the rent savings.

## The Transition

If you're moving from storage to flex space, know what to expect:

Rent goes up. $250 per month storage becomes $1,600 to $1,800 for flex. That's real. But you gain back 5 to 10 hours per week.

You'll have more space than you need at first. A 1,000 SF bay might feel huge compared to a 200 SF storage unit. You'll fill it. Don't worry about maximizing square footage at the start.

You'll run it differently. With 24/7 access and parking, your crew can show up early, grab tools, and go. No more "we have to hit the storage unit before we start." You're faster.

## Why Contractors Are Making the Move

The calculus is simple: a contractor who was paying $250 per month storage plus 10 hours per week in drive time is losing $500 to $600 per week in productivity. Moving to a $1,800 per month flex space costs $600 more per month but saves $2,000 to $2,400 per month in recovered billable hours.

That's not even accounting for the reliability gain. With power, parking, and 24/7 access, the contractor runs a more professional operation.

## FAQ

**Q: Is a storage unit really that much of a time sink?**
A: Yes, if you're using it daily. Two trips a day (morning grab, evening stock) at 15 minutes each is 30 minutes daily, 2.5 hours per week. Over a year that's 130 hours of driving to a storage unit instead of working. At $50 per hour, that's $6,500 in lost income.

**Q: Can I get electrical hookup at a storage facility?**
A: Rarely. Most storage facilities don't have power available to individual units. Some newer facilities offer it as a premium add-on ($100 to $150 monthly), but you're still limited to outdoor outlet use and you lose all the other flex space benefits (parking, hours, door size).

**Q: What if I need climate control?**
A: Flex space buildings don't typically offer climate control (heating and air conditioning). If you're storing sensitive goods, this is a real constraint. But most contractors don't need it; they need electrical, parking, and 24/7 access. Talk to the landlord about what you're storing and whether flex space works.
