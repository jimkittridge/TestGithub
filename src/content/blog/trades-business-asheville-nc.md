Running a trades business in Asheville means juggling permits, licensing, insurance, and finding space that actually works for your operation. Here's what you need to know.

## Where Your Jurisdiction Actually Is

Asheville city has one set of rules. Buncombe County has another. Unincorporated areas outside Asheville (Fletcher, Arden, Hendersonville) have county rules, but some have local ordinances too.

This matters because licensing, permits, and inspections follow jurisdiction lines. An electrician licensed in Asheville city can work in Asheville city and unincorporated Buncombe County. But if you're pulling permits, you need to know which jurisdiction you're in.

Most trades businesses base in Fletcher or Arden (unincorporated Buncombe County) because zoning is straightforward and rent is lower than Asheville proper. Work might be citywide, but your base is in the county.

## NC Licensing Requirements

North Carolina requires licensing for electricians, plumbers, and HVAC contractors. Licensing is state-level, but you need to register with the NC Licensing Board.

**Electricians**: Must hold a journeyman license (at minimum 8,000 hours experience plus exam). Master electrician requires additional hours and exam. A journeyman can run a solo business; you don't need a master license, but many do.

**Plumbers**: Must hold a journeyman license (8,000 hours plus exam). Same structure as electrical. Most independent plumbers are journeymen.

**HVAC Contractors**: Must hold a contractor license. This requires work experience and passing the NCHPACA exam. Unlike electrical and plumbing, HVAC is contractor-level only; there's no separate journeyman path.

**General Contractors**: No state license required in NC if you're doing work under $30,000. Above that, you need General Contractor licensing from the NC Licensing Board. Most contractors operating in Asheville do work over $30,000 frequently, so they're licensed.

Check your trade's specific requirements with the [NC Licensing Board](https://www.nclatiservices.org/).

## Buncombe County Permitting

For plumbing, electrical, or HVAC work in Buncombe County, you'll need permits. You pull permits through the county's Development Services office or the Asheville city office if the work is in the city.

Permits cost $100 to $300 depending on scope. They take 5 to 10 business days to issue. Some routine work (like replacing a water heater) is fast-tracked. Major rewires or new construction are slower.

## Signage and Your Space

If you're operating from a flex bay in Fletcher or Arden, you can usually put a company sign on the building or bay door. Check your lease. Most industrial landlords allow it; some require approval. A small sign (2x3 feet) is typically fine.

Parking: make sure clients and inspectors can park. An inspector doing an electrical inspection needs to get out of their car and walk to the work area. Your bay needs at least 2 to 3 parking spots.

Hours: trades work starts early. Most contractors are out by 6 or 7 AM and back by 4 or 5 PM. 24/7 access to your flex bay matters; you're loading trucks at 5:30 AM and unloading at 5:15 PM.

## Insurance and Bonding

General liability insurance is mandatory. Expect $500 to $1,200 per year for a solo trades contractor. Coverage should be $1 million minimum ($2 million is safer).

Workers compensation insurance is required if you have employees. For a solo operator, you often exempt yourself. Cost for one employee is $1,500 to $3,000 annually depending on trade and safety record.

Bonding is required for some work (government contracts, large private projects). A bonding company issues a bond that guarantees your work. Cost is 1 to 3 percent of the contract value. If a client pays you for a $10,000 plumbing job and you don't finish, the bond pays them. For most small trades work, bonding isn't required, but ask your clients.

## Business Structure

Sole proprietor: simple, cheap, minimal paperwork. You're personally liable for lawsuits. Most trades start here.

LLC (Limited Liability Company): costs $100 to $300 to form in NC. Provides liability protection; clients can sue the business, not you personally. Recommended once you have revenue.

S-Corp or C-Corp: more complex accounting, rarely necessary for a trades business unless you're running a larger operation.

Talk to an accountant. For a trades business in Asheville, sole proprietor or LLC is typical.

## The Asheville Economy and Trades Work

Tourism drives a lot of trades demand. Hotels, restaurants, Airbnbs, and vacation homes need maintenance, repair, and upgrades. Much of that work happens in shoulder seasons (spring and fall) and off-season (winter). Summer is busy with tourism, but many contractors are booked solid.

Construction and rebuild (post-Helene) is the dominant driver through 2026 and probably into 2027. New construction, remodels, emergency repairs all need trades.

Mountain weather is a factor. Winter brings ice, freezing temps, and short days. HVAC contractors are busiest in winter. Plumbers deal with freeze-outs. Roofers wait for summer. Structure your pricing and scheduling around these cycles.

## Space Requirements by Trade

**Electricians**: Need space for tools, materials, maybe a truck. 1,000 SF is sufficient. Power requirements are moderate (you might run a compressor, charger, table saw). Parking for your van and customer vehicles.

**Plumbers**: More material-intensive. Need space for pipe, fixtures, tools, maybe a work bench for small assembly. 1,000 to 1,500 SF. Water nearby is nice but not essential. Parking for your van and customer vehicles.

**HVAC**: Need space for units (sometimes large), tools, testing equipment, paperwork. 1,500 to 2,000 SF. Electrical for diagnostic equipment. Parking.

**General Contractors**: Varies widely. Some run project management out of a small office with a truck. Others stage materials and equipment. 1,000 to 2,000 SF is typical. Drive-in door for larger materials. 200 amp electrical if you're running tools or charging batteries. Parking for multiple vehicles.

## Hiring Your First Employee

Employees complicate things. You need workers comp insurance, payroll taxes, and a clear employment agreement. Tax liability increases. But one employee effectively doubles your capacity.

For a solo operator making $5,000 to $6,000 per month, hire a part-time apprentice or helper first. Pay cash or 1099 (watch IRS rules on this) and keep overhead low. Once you're consistently over $8,000 per month, hire a full-time W-2 employee.

## Common Mistakes

**Too small a space**: You under-estimate tool storage, material inventory, and parking needs. Get 1,000 to 1,500 SF even if you think you need 500. You'll use it.

**Not budgeting for admin time**: You're running a business, not just doing trades. Permits, inspections, scheduling, billing, taxes. Set aside 5 to 10 hours per week.

**Ignoring seasonal cycles**: Asheville trades work is lumpy. Summer is booked. January and February are slow. Price high in peak, save during slow, and budget accordingly.

**Neglecting relationships**: Most trades work comes from repeat clients and referrals. Deliver exceptional work, keep clients happy, ask for referrals. Marketing is secondary to execution.

**Underbidding**: New trades operators often quote low to win work. Stop. Your time and expertise have value. Bid to cover costs plus 40 to 50 percent markup.

## FAQ

**Q: Do I need a contractor license to run a solo plumbing or electrical business?**
A: You need a journeyman license to do plumbing or electrical work in North Carolina. You don't need a separate "contractor" license unless you're a general contractor. Check the NC Licensing Board for your specific trade.

**Q: Can I run a trades business out of my house?**
A: Asheville and Buncombe County zoning generally don't allow commercial operations from residential addresses. You can run an office, but you can't stage materials, park multiple work vehicles, or have client traffic. Get a flex bay. Rent is cheap compared to licensing and insurance headaches if code enforcement finds you.

**Q: What if I'm buying tools and equipment; can I deduct that?**
A: Tools lasting more than one year are typically depreciated over time, not deducted immediately. Small tools under $2,500 can often be expensed immediately. Consult an accountant; the rules change. Keeping receipts and tracking everything is critical for your tax return.
